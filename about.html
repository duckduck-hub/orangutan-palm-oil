# Orangutans ## Species Information Orangutans belong to the genus *Pongo*, which includes three extant species: the Bornean orangutan (*Pongo pygmaeus*), the Sumatran orangutan (*Pongo abelii*), and the newly discovered Tapanuli orangutan (*Pongo tapanuliensis*). These primates are native to the rainforests of Indonesia and Malaysia. ## Characteristics Orangutans are the largest arboreal mammals, characterized by their long arms and reddish-brown fur. An adult male can weigh up to 200 pounds and reach a height of 4-5 feet, while the females are smaller, weighing around 100 pounds. They have distinctive facial features, including a wide, flat nose and cheek pads in mature males, which are associated with sexual maturity. ## Behavior Orangutans are predominantly solitary animals, unlike many other primate species. They are highly intelligent and exhibit complex behaviors, such as tool use and problem-solving. They communicate through a variety of vocalizations and gestures, which serve different social purposes in their environment. Their home range can vary significantly based on the availability of food and other resources. ## Diet Orangutans are primarily frugivorous, relying heavily on fruits for sustenance, particularly ripe figs and durians. They also consume leaves, bark, flowers, and insects. Their foraging habits have a significant impact on their rainforest habitat, as they help in seed dispersal, promoting biodiversity in their ecosystem. ## Conservation Status Orangutans are classified as critically endangered by the IUCN Red List. The primary threats to their survival are habitat destruction due to logging, palm oil plantations, and poaching. Conservation efforts are crucial to protect their natural habitats and ensure their survival for future generations. Various NGOs and governments are working to establish protected areas and promote sustainable practices to mitigate the threats faced by these great apes.
